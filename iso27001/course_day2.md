ISO 27001 Certification Course: Day 2 Notes

## The Three Pillars of ISO Standards

Modern ISO standards, although they address different domains, share a common conceptual structure. Understanding this structure makes it easier to read and interpret not only ISO 27001, but also many other management systems, such as those related to quality, environment, occupational health and safety, or artificial intelligence.

The three common pillars are:

- Process approach
- Risk-based thinking
- High Level Structure

---

## First Pillar: Process Approach

The process approach consists of viewing the organisation not as a simple hierarchical pyramid, but as a set of interconnected processes.

Each process receives inputs, performs transformation activities and produces outputs intended for other processes or for the final customer.

---

## Second Pillar: Risk-Based Thinking

The second pillar is risk-based thinking.

In the ISO logic, risk should not be understood only as something negative. Risk is the effect of uncertainty on objectives and may therefore appear both as a threat and as an opportunity.

This means that an organisation should not simply avoid problems, but should also be able to recognise favourable opportunities that may arise from uncertain situations.

Risk therefore becomes a planning tool, not only a danger to be feared.

### Root Cause Logic

In the ISO approach, “chance” does not exist.

If an incident, a nonconformity, a complaint or an operational issue occurs, the organisation should ask what the root cause of the event is.

It should not stop at the visible effect, but should reconstruct the process that generated the issue.

Only in this way is it possible to act effectively and prevent the same event from happening again.

### Controlling the Unpredictable

Risk-based thinking requires the organisation to prepare even for what appears to be unpredictable.

Not everything can be directly controlled, but many consequences can be anticipated, mitigated or managed.

An organisation can control:

- its level of preparation;
- the robustness of its processes;
- the quality of its procedures;
- personnel training;
- its incident response capability.

In this sense, risk-based thinking does not eliminate uncertainty, but requires the organisation to reduce and govern it as much as possible.

---

## Third Pillar: High Level Structure

The third pillar is the High Level Structure, which is the common high-level structure used by modern ISO standards.

This structure makes standards easier to integrate with each other, because many chapters follow the same logic and the same order.

The High Level Structure allows anyone familiar with one ISO standard to navigate other ISO standards more easily.

### High Level Structure Chapters

| Chapter | Topic |
|---|---|
| 4 | Context of the organisation |
| 5 | Leadership |
| 6 | Planning |
| 7 | Support |
| 8 | Operation |
| 9 | Performance evaluation |
| 10 | Improvement |

Chapter 4 concerns the context of the organisation, meaning the understanding of the internal and external environment in which the company operates.

Chapter 5 concerns leadership and the role of top management.

Chapter 6 deals with planning, including the management of risks and opportunities.

Chapter 7 concerns support resources, such as competence, awareness, communication and documented information.

Chapter 8 describes operational activities.

Chapter 9 concerns performance evaluation.

Chapter 10 is dedicated to continual improvement.

---

## Exam Note

For the exam, it is important to be able to list and briefly explain the three ISO pillars:

### Process Approach

The organisation is viewed as a set of interconnected processes, each with inputs, activities, outputs and responsibilities.

### Risk-Based Thinking

The organisation must identify risks and opportunities, starting from the assumption that events have identifiable and manageable causes.

### High Level Structure

Modern ISO standards share a common structure, based on chapters 4 to 10, which facilitates the integration and understanding of standards.

# The Triangle of Objective Evidence

The auditor collects objective evidence mainly through three sources.

## 1. What the Auditor Hears

The first source consists of what the auditor hears.

During interviews, the auditor collects:

- statements;
- explanations;
- operational descriptions;
- answers provided by the audited organisation.

However, verbal statements alone are not always sufficient. They must be compared with other evidence, especially when the standard requires documented evidence or when possible inconsistencies emerge.

## 2. What the Auditor Observes

The second source is what the auditor directly observes.

During the company visit, the auditor observes:

- environments;
- behaviours;
- operational methods;
- workstations;
- notice boards;
- configurations;
- physical areas;
- tools used.

Direct observation makes it possible to verify whether what is stated during interviews corresponds to reality.

## 3. Documented Information

The third source is documented information.

Documented information may have any format or medium, including:

- paper documents;
- digital files;
- records;
- logs;
- screenshots;
- procedures;
- reports;
- emails;
- configurations;
- any other form of evidence retained by the organisation.

Some clauses of the standard explicitly require documented information. In these cases, a simple verbal statement is not sufficient, because the standard requires documented evidence.

---

# Auditor Behaviour

## Questions during the Audit

### The Importance of Open Questions

The type of questions used during the audit is fundamental.

Closed questions should preferably be avoided, meaning questions that can be answered simply with “yes” or “no”.

These questions block dialogue and produce little useful information.

Open questions, on the other hand, allow the interviewee to explain their work, describe processes and provide details.

They are therefore more effective for collecting objective evidence and truly understanding how the organisation works.

An open question may be formulated simply, for example:

> “Can you explain how you analyse risks and opportunities in the company?”

This type of question invites the interviewee to speak and allows the auditor to listen, investigate further and identify possible inconsistencies.

---

# Conformities, Opportunities for Improvement and Nonconformities

## Conformities

The first possible audit result is conformity.

A conformity occurs when a requirement is satisfied.

Conformities must be written in the report, not only critical issues.

The report should therefore also include positive statements, for example:

> “Requirement 5.2 is fulfilled, as the policy is effectively written and communicated.”

In a well-conducted audit, most findings should be conformities.

---

## Nonconformities

A nonconformity is the failure to satisfy a requirement.

To declare a nonconformity, the auditor must always connect the objective evidence to a specific requirement of the standard.

Nonconformities are divided into:

- minor nonconformities;
- major nonconformities.

A nonconformity is major when it puts the management system at risk or endangers the information security of the customer or of other relevant interested parties, such as authorities, employees or suppliers.

A nonconformity is minor when there is a failure to satisfy a requirement, but the issue does not compromise the system as a whole and does not immediately put relevant information at risk.

---

# How to Write the Audit Report

## The Four Fundamental Elements of the Report

A report must be clear, understandable and technically correct.

Each audit finding must contain four fundamental elements:

1. description of the objective evidence;
2. clause of the standard;
3. statement of conformity or nonconformity;
4. justification, only in case of nonconformity.

These elements may be organised in a table or in a bullet list.

---

## First Element: Description of Objective Evidence

### Evidence Heard and Observed

The description of objective evidence must clearly explain what the auditor’s assessment is based on.

If the evidence comes from an interview, it may be written that the organisation “stated that...”.

If the evidence comes from direct observation, it may be written that “it was observed that...”.

Examples:

- “The organisation stated that the policy is communicated through posting on the notice board.”
- “It was observed that the policy was not present on the indicated notice board.”
- “It was observed that a post-it note containing the administrator password was present on an employee’s workstation.”

The description must be factual, not emotional.

The auditor must not write generic judgments, but must report what was heard, seen or verified.

---

## Documentary Evidence

When the objective evidence consists of documented information, the report must precisely indicate:

- document code;
- document name;
- date;
- revision number.

Example:

> “Information Security Policy, rev. 2 dated 15 February 2026.”

These elements are fundamental because, in ISO 27001, the integrity of documented information is particularly relevant.

If it is not known which version of the document was verified, it is not possible to demonstrate with certainty that it was the latest valid version.

---

## Second Element: Clause of the Standard

The second element of the report is the reference to the regulatory requirement used as the audit criterion.

It is sufficient to indicate the clause number of the standard, for example:

- 5.2 for the policy;
- 6.1.3 for risk treatment;
- 7.2 for competence;
- 9.1 for monitoring and evaluation.

It is also possible to add the title of the clause, especially to make the report clearer for the audited organisation, but the essential element is the requirement number.

The clause of the standard demonstrates that the auditor’s assessment is not based on a personal opinion, but on the comparison between objective evidence and audit criteria.

---

## Third Element: Statement of Conformity or Nonconformity

### Statement of Conformity

If the requirement is satisfied, the auditor writes a statement of conformity.

Possible formulas include:

- “The process is compliant.”
- “The process is effective.”
- “The requirement is satisfied.”

In this case, the finding ends here. No further justification is necessary.

---

### Statement of Minor Nonconformity

If the auditor identifies a minor nonconformity, formulas such as the following should be used:

- “The process is not fully compliant.”
- “The process is not fully effective.”

This expression indicates that the requirement is not fully satisfied, but that the issue does not compromise the entire management system.

---

### Statement of Major Nonconformity

If the nonconformity is major, the language must be more direct:

- “The process is non-compliant.”
- “The process is ineffective.”

---

## Fourth Element: Justification of the Nonconformity

### When the Field Remains Empty

The fourth element is completed only if a nonconformity is detected.

If the result is a conformity, this field remains empty.

---

### Justification of a Minor Nonconformity

In the case of a minor nonconformity, the justification must explain why the issue is not severe enough to compromise the system.

Suggested formulas:

> “The nonconformity is minor because the information security management system is not compromised.”

Or:

> “The nonconformity is minor because the information security of the customer or of other relevant interested parties is not put at risk.”

These formulas help clearly distinguish a minor nonconformity from a major one.

---

### Justification of a Major Nonconformity

In the case of a major nonconformity, the justification follows the opposite logic.

Suggested formulas:

> “The nonconformity is major because the information security management system is compromised.”

Or:

> “The nonconformity is major because the information security of the customer or of other relevant interested parties is put at risk.”

The difference between minor and major does not therefore depend on the length of the finding or on the auditor’s personal impression, but on the impact of the nonconformity on the system and on the information of interested parties.

---

# Practical Study Scheme

## Structure of an Audit Finding

| Element | What to write |
|---|---|
| Objective evidence | What was stated, observed or verified through documentation |
| Requirement | Clause number of the standard |
| Assessment | Compliant, not fully compliant, non-compliant |
| Justification | Only if there is a minor or major nonconformity |

---

## Example of a Conforming Finding

**Objective evidence:**  
The Information Security Policy, document POL-SI-01, revision 2 dated 15 February 2026, was verified. The policy is approved by management and communicated internally through the company intranet.

**Clause of the standard:**  
5.2

**Statement:**  
The requirement is compliant.

**Justification:**  
Not applicable.

---

## Example of a Minor Nonconformity

**Objective evidence:**  
The competence management procedure, document HR-SEC-02, revision 1 dated 10 January 2026, was verified. During the interview with the HR manager, it emerged that information security training was provided to new hires, but records relating to two employees hired during the previous month had not yet been completed.

**Clause of the standard:**  
7.2

**Statement:**  
The process is not fully compliant.

**Justification:**  
The nonconformity is minor because the information security management system is not compromised.

---

## Example of a Major Nonconformity

**Objective evidence:**  
During the document review, the statement of applicability relating to the information security management system was not made available.

**Clause of the standard:**  
6.1.3

**Statement:**  
The process is non-compliant.

**Justification:**  
The nonconformity is major because the information security management system is compromised.

# Opportunities for Improvement in Audits

## What Opportunities for Improvement Are

In some contexts, these are also called “observations”.

Opportunities for improvement are findings that the auditor may include in the report when an aspect is identified that, although compliant, could be improved.

They are therefore neither pure conformities nor nonconformities.

They are indications suggesting a possible positive evolution of the system.

Their use is strongly discouraged.

---

# System Audits and Process Audits

## System Audit

The ISO 27001 logic is based on a system audit approach.

In this type of audit, the objective is to verify whether the information security management system complies with the requirements of the standard.

The main findings are therefore:

- conformities;
- minor nonconformities;
- major nonconformities.

The assessment is essentially binary: the requirement is satisfied or it is not satisfied.

If it is not satisfied, the auditor must determine whether the nonconformity is minor or major based on its impact on the management system or on interested parties.

---

## Process Audit

There are also process audits, which are typical of some quality or sector-specific schemes.

In these audits, maturity levels may be used instead of only the compliant/non-compliant distinction.

---

# Meetings during the Audit

## Types of Meetings

The course also introduces the different types of meetings that may take place during an audit.

The most important are:

- opening meeting;
- closing meeting;
- end-of-day meetings;
- progress meetings;
- internal audit team meetings.

The opening meeting and closing meeting are always necessary.

The other meetings are organised depending on:

- the complexity of the audit;
- the duration of the audit;
- the number of auditors involved;
- the presence of technical experts or observers.

---

# Opening Meeting and Closing Meeting

## Minimum Necessary Meetings

The opening meeting formally starts the audit, confirms the plan, introduces the team and clarifies operational arrangements.

The closing meeting presents the audit results, including conformities, any nonconformities and general conclusions.

It is the moment in which the organisation receives the official overview of what emerged.

These two meetings are the minimum necessary elements in any well-conducted audit.

Even when time is limited, they should not be eliminated.

---

## Additional Meetings

End-of-day meetings or progress meetings are particularly useful in long audits, lasting several days or involving large teams.

They are used to:

- review the evidence collected;
- coordinate the auditors;
- verify whether the plan needs to be adapted.

Internal team meetings may be useful when it is necessary to consult a technical expert or discuss the correct classification of a finding.

However, the instructor recommends using them sparingly, because the audit plan must still respect the planned duration and should not unnecessarily burden the organisation.

---

# The Role of the Team Leader

## Specific Responsibilities

The team leader has greater responsibilities than the other members of the audit team.

This figure coordinates the entire audit, manages the main communications, leads the meetings and maintains control of the audit plan.

The team leader is responsible for:

- coordinating the team;
- managing any changes to the plan;
- officially speaking on behalf of the team;
- leading the opening meeting;
- leading the closing meeting;
- coordinating the drafting of the report;
- ensuring that the activities are consistent with the audit objectives.

If, during the audit, it becomes necessary to modify the plan, change the order of interviews or reorganise activities, the decision must go through the team leader.

This figure therefore ensures consistency, control and unity in the conduct of the audit.

---

# Questions the Team Leader Must Ask

## Existence and Completeness of the System

The first question concerns the existence of the system itself.

Has the organisation actually prepared a management system capable of addressing all clauses and requirements of the standard?

This question is fundamental because certification does not concern isolated documents, but the entire system.

The auditor must therefore ask whether the organisation has built a coherent structure capable of covering all applicable ISO 27001 requirements.

---

## Implementation

The second question concerns implementation.

Does the system exist only on paper, or is it actually put into practice?

A management system cannot be limited to written procedures, approved policies or formal documents.

It must be applied in the organisation’s daily life.

---

## Effectiveness

The third question concerns effectiveness.

Is the system achieving its objectives?

In other words, it is not enough for activities to be performed; it is necessary to verify whether they produce useful results consistent with the purpose of the management system.

This distinction between implementation and effectiveness is central in Stage 2 audits and surveillance audits.

---

# Scope and Objective

## Scope of Application and Purpose

The scope is the field of application.

It indicates the perimeter within which the management system is applied, including:

- sites;
- activities;
- services;
- processes;
- organisational units;
- system boundaries.

The objective, on the other hand, is the purpose of the audit.

It indicates the reason why the audit is carried out.

For example, in an ISO 27001 certification audit, the objective may be to verify the conformity of the information security management system with the requirements of the standard.

---

# The Closing Meeting

## Participants and Contents

The closing meeting should normally involve the same participants who attended the opening meeting.

This makes it possible to formally close the audit cycle in front of the same figures who took part in the start of the activities.

During the closing meeting, the team leader must:

- recap the audit objective;
- recall the audit criterion, meaning the applicable standard;
- present the results;
- read or summarise the report;
- explain any nonconformities;
- clarify treatment timelines.

Even if it may seem repetitive, during the closing meeting it is necessary to restate why the audit was carried out.

Example:

> “We are here to verify the conformity of your information security management system with the requirements of ISO 27001.”

This maintains the connection between the evidence collected, the regulatory criterion and the audit conclusions.

---

# Nonconformities and Certification

Even a single minor nonconformity may prevent the certificate from being issued or may cause technical suspension until it is treated.

To be compliant with ISO 27001, the organisation must satisfy all applicable requirements of the standard.

It is not possible to ignore one requirement only because all the others are fulfilled.

---

# Problem Solving for Nonconformities

When a nonconformity is issued, the organisation must treat it through a structured problem-solving process.

Problem solving prevents the organisation from correcting the issue only superficially.

The objective is not only to “fix what is wrong”, but to identify the cause of the issue and prevent it from recurring.

---

# The Five Steps of Problem Solving

## First Step: Containment Action or Correction

The first step is containment action, also called correction.

This activity acts on the immediate effect of the problem.

It is essential not to confuse correction with corrective action.

Correction eliminates or contains the visible effect, but does not necessarily resolve the cause that generated the nonconformity.

---

## Second Step: Root Cause Analysis

The second step is root cause analysis.

In this phase, the organisation must understand why the nonconformity occurred.

The ISO logic starts from the principle that chance does not exist: every problem derives from one or more causes.

The organisation must therefore go beyond the superficial effect and identify the true origin of the nonconformity.

---

## Third Step: Corrective Action

The third step is corrective action.

It must not be confused with the initial correction.

While correction acts on the effect, corrective action acts on the cause.

Once the root cause has been identified, the corrective action should be built as the logical negation of the cause itself.

Examples:

- If the cause is lack of awareness of the process owner, the corrective action may be targeted training.
- If the cause is lack of document control, the corrective action may be the introduction of a periodic control.

Corrective action therefore has a preventive function: it prevents the same nonconformity from occurring again.

---

## Fourth Step: Implementation of the Corrective Action

The fourth step is the implementation of the corrective action.

It is not enough to decide what to do: it must actually be done.

Each step should have:

- a responsible person;
- a team or people involved;
- a planned date;
- evidence of implementation.

This step is essential because many organisations fail precisely when transforming decisions into concrete actions.

---

## Fifth Step: Verification of Effectiveness

The fifth step is verification of effectiveness.

After implementing the corrective action, the organisation must verify whether it actually worked.

The verification may be carried out through:

- internal audit;
- statistical control;
- testing;
- document review;
- monitoring of indicators;
- operational verification.

This step demonstrates whether the nonconformity has actually been resolved.

---

# Timelines for Closing Nonconformities

## Closure within 60 Days

Nonconformities must be managed within 60 days.

This means that the organisation must carry out the necessary activities to treat the nonconformity and submit the required evidence within this deadline.

The auditor must not perform the problem solving, but must verify its adequacy.

---

## Difference between Minor and Major Nonconformities

In the case of a minor nonconformity, the organisation performs all five steps and the auditor verifies effectiveness the following year, during the next audit.

In the case of a major nonconformity, the organisation performs the first four steps within 60 days, but the fifth step, verification of effectiveness, must be performed by the auditor within the same period.

This is because a major nonconformity puts the system at risk and cannot be left without verification until the following year.

If the verification shows that the corrective action was not effective, it is necessary to go back to the root cause analysis.

This is because the ineffectiveness of the action may depend on two issues:

- the identified root cause was wrong;
- the corrective action was poorly defined or implemented.

---

# Recurrence and Severity

## Why Recurrence Is Serious

Recurrence is considered particularly serious because it shows that the previous problem-solving process did not work.

If an issue already detected occurs again, it means that the organisation did not correctly identify the root cause or did not implement effective corrective actions.

For this reason, repetition of the same nonconformity may lead to a more severe classification.

It is no longer only the original issue, but also the inability of the management system to learn and improve.

---

# Study Scheme: Problem Solving in Five Steps

| Step | Name | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Correction / Containment | Acts on the immediate effect |
| 2 | Root cause analysis | Identifies why the issue occurred |
| 3 | Corrective action | Acts on the cause to prevent recurrence |
| 4 | Implementation | Applies the corrective action concretely |
| 5 | Verification of effectiveness | Checks whether the action worked |

---

# The Importance of the Report

## The Report as a Summary of Audit Activity

A good report must be:

- clear;
- understandable;
- consistent;
- based on objective evidence;
- connected to specific requirements of the standard;
- written with technical but readable language.

The auditor must not write personal impressions or generic judgments.

The auditor must describe what was verified, indicate the applicable requirement, state the result and, if necessary, justify the severity of the nonconformity.

---

# Use of the Standard during the Exam and Audit

## How to Identify the Clause of the Standard

What is truly important is knowing the High Level Structure and being able to place the issue in the correct chapter.

Examples:

- If the evidence concerns competence, the reference is Chapter 7, dedicated to support.
- If the evidence concerns risks and opportunities, the reference is Chapter 6.
- If the evidence concerns improvement and nonconformity management, the reference is Chapter 10.

Once the correct chapter has been identified, the index of the standard can be consulted to identify the specific requirement.

---

# The Closing Meeting

## Presentation of Results

The explanation of the report is directly connected to the closing meeting.

During this meeting, the team leader must present the audit results and explain any nonconformities.

However, it is important to remember that the auditor must not perform problem solving on behalf of the organisation.

The auditor’s responsibility is to:

- present the finding;
- explain the classification;
- clarify timelines;
- ensure that the organisation has understood the meaning of the findings.

The organisation’s responsibility is to:

- analyse the cause;
- define corrective actions;
- implement them;
- verify their effectiveness;
- provide evidence of closure.

This boundary is fundamental: the auditor verifies, but does not solve the problem for the company.

---

# Agreement on Reporting

## Signature and Acceptance of the Report

In many schemes, including ISO 27001, a form of agreement on reporting is required.

This means that the report or the findings must be shared with the organisation and, often, signed or formally accepted.

The signature means that the report has been presented and that the organisation has acknowledged it.

This step is important because it prevents later disputes about the content or communication of the findings.

---

# Final Clarifications in the Closing Meeting

## Verifying that Everything Has Been Understood

During the closing meeting, it is appropriate to leave space for questions from the organisation.

The auditor may ask whether everything is clear, whether further explanations are needed or whether there are points to clarify.

This moment is important because the closing meeting should not be a cold and mechanical reading of the report.

It should be a professional discussion in which the organisation clearly understands:

- what results emerged;
- which nonconformities were detected;
- which timelines must be respected;
- what will happen after the audit.

The auditor must therefore ensure that no ambiguity remains, especially when nonconformities are present.

---

# Study Scheme: Closing Meeting

## What the Auditor Must Do

During the closing meeting, the auditor must:

- summarise the audit objective;
- recall the criterion used;
- present the findings;
- explain any nonconformities;
- clarify treatment timelines;
- remind the organisation that problem solving is its responsibility;
- verify that everything has been understood;
- have the report signed or accepted, if required;
- clarify that the audit is based on sampling;
- thank the organisation;
- highlight any positive aspects.

## What the Auditor Must Not Do

The auditor must not:

- provide consultancy;
- define corrective actions on behalf of the company;
- solve the nonconformity;
- transmit anxiety or guilt;
- present the report aggressively;
- imply that the absence of nonconformities means absolute perfection.

---

# The Audit Report

## Main Contents of the Report

The course returns to the topic of the audit report, clarifying which information must appear in the final document.

The report is not only a narrative summary of the audit, but the official document that records:

- objectives;
- scope;
- criteria;
- participants;
- findings;
- conclusions.

The audit report must first include the audit objectives, meaning the reason why the verification was carried out.

It must then indicate the scope, meaning the organisational, physical, technological or procedural perimeter subjected to audit.

The reference standard must also be indicated. In this course, this is ISO 27001, which represents the criterion against which the collected evidence is assessed.

The report must also include:

- customer identification data;
- audit team;
- participants;
- audit dates;
- results;
- conclusions;
- statements of conformity.

In some schemes, a separate document dedicated to nonconformities may also be produced, to make it easier for the organisation to manage subsequent actions.

---

# Distribution of the Audit Report

## When to Deliver the Report

In the ISO 27001 context, the audit report should be distributed, if possible, during the closing meeting.

This allows the organisation to immediately receive the official overview of the findings and start managing any nonconformities.

If it is not possible to deliver the report immediately, it must still be transmitted within an agreed and as short as possible timeframe.

This aspect is important because the 60 days available to close any nonconformities start from the date of the closing meeting, that is, from the last day of the audit.

A delay in the delivery of the report must be justified and communicated to the organisation.

---

# Audit Follow-Up

## Subsequent Control of Actions

After the audit, a follow-up may be planned.

A follow-up is a subsequent control aimed at verifying progress or the correct planning of subsequent activities.

In many cases, follow-up can be managed by:

- email;
- phone;
- videoconference.

The follow-up does not necessarily coincide with the complete closure of the nonconformity.

It is used to verify that the organisation has correctly prepared the plan to manage it.

In particular, the auditor may check that:

- the system manager has understood the finding;
- the action plan has been properly drafted;
- the closure path is consistent with what is required.

---

# Information Security Properties

## The RID / CIA Triad

RID means:

| Italian Acronym | Italian Term | English Acronym | English Term |
|---|---|---|---|
| R | Riservatezza | C | Confidentiality |
| I | Integrità | I | Integrity |
| D | Disponibilità | A | Availability |

All security controls and all audit activities must be connected to one or more of these properties.

When assessing a risk, analysing a security measure or verifying a control, it is always necessary to ask which property of information is being protected.

### Confidentiality

Confidentiality concerns the protection of information from unauthorised access.

### Integrity

Integrity concerns the correctness, completeness and reliability of information.

### Availability

Availability concerns the possibility of accessing information and systems when needed.

A control may act on only one property or on multiple properties at the same time.

For example, a backup mainly protects availability, but it may also contribute to integrity if it allows correct data to be restored after corruption.

---

# Verification of Previous Year Nonconformities

A very important point is the inclusion, in the audit plan, of the verification of the previous year’s nonconformities.

If minor nonconformities were detected during the previous audit, the auditor must allocate time to verify the effectiveness of their closure.

If the previous nonconformity was minor, the verification of effectiveness normally takes place during the following audit.

If it was major, the verification should already have been carried out within the required 60 days.

---

# Connection between Audit Plan and Process Mapping

## Why Mapping Is Essential

Without mapping the organisation’s business processes, it is not possible to correctly build the audit plan.

The audit plan must indicate which processes will be verified.

To do this, the auditor must know which processes exist in the organisation.

If the auditor does not know the process map, they cannot know whether the company has processes such as:

- production;
- maintenance;
- procurement;
- logistics;
- human resources;
- industrialisation;
- management system.

Process mapping therefore becomes the logical basis of the plan.

It makes it possible to identify:

- which processes must be audited;
- who the process owners are;
- which interactions exist between processes;
- which activities must be included in the agenda;
- how much time is needed for the verification.

# Scope, Criterion and Data in the Audit Plan

## Elements to Include in the Plan

The audit plan must also include the scope and the audit criterion.

The scope describes the perimeter subject to verification.

The criterion indicates the standard or scheme against which the audit is conducted.

In the case of ISO 27001, the criterion will be ISO/IEC 27001.

The plan must also contain:

- the address or references of the audited organisation;
- the audit team;
- the dates.

# The Turtle Diagram

## What the Turtle Diagram Is

The turtle diagram, used in German quality approaches, is a tool for representing a process in a structured way.

It visualises all the elements needed to understand how the process works.

The name derives from the shape of the diagram: the central process resembles the shell of a turtle, while the elements connected to the process are represented as the head, tail and legs.

---

## Elements of the Diagram

In the turtle diagram:

- the central body represents the process;
- the tail represents the inputs;
- the head represents the outputs;
- the rear legs represent resources and methods;
- the front legs represent responsibilities and indicators.

This model helps the auditor verify a process completely.

The auditor does not simply ask “what do you do?”, but analyses:

- where the process starts from;
- which resources it uses;
- which methods it follows;
- who is responsible;
- which outputs it produces;
- how performance is monitored.

The turtle diagram is therefore a very useful tool for applying the process approach concretely.

# Assessing Implementation and Effectiveness

During audit preparation and execution, the auditor must assess not only the formal existence of the system, but also its level of implementation and effectiveness.

## Implementation

Implementation concerns whether the planned activities have actually been carried out.

An organisation may have procedures, policies and plans, but the auditor must verify whether these elements are actually applied in practice.

## Effectiveness

Effectiveness concerns the system’s ability to achieve the intended results.

A process may be formally implemented but fail to produce the expected results.

In that case, the auditor must assess how the organisation analyses the failure to achieve objectives and which actions it decides to take.

Failure to achieve an objective is not automatically a nonconformity.

It becomes an issue if the organisation does not analyse the result, does not understand the causes and does not plan appropriate actions.

# Minor and Major Nonconformities

## Further Formulation of the Difference

The transcript also returns to the distinction between minor and major nonconformities.

A minor nonconformity does not impair the ability of the management system to achieve the intended results.

This means that there is a failure to satisfy a requirement, but the system as a whole remains capable of functioning and achieving its objectives.

A major nonconformity, on the other hand, affects the ability of the management system to achieve the expected results.

In this case, the issue is more serious because it compromises the system or puts at risk the information of the customer or other relevant interested parties.

# When There Is Not Enough Evidence to Assess

If, during an audit, the auditor believes that there is not enough evidence to express an assessment, this can be stated.

In this case, the auditor must not force either a conformity or a nonconformity.

Instead, the auditor must clearly explain why the available evidence does not allow an assessment to be made.
