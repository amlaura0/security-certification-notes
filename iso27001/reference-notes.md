# ISO 27001 Reference Notes
---

## 1. Types of Audits

ISO management system audits may be conducted as first-party, second-party or third-party audits.

### First-Party Audits

First-party audits are internal audits performed by an organisation on its own management system.

They are generally less formal than certification audits and may focus on:

- internal process conformity;
- effectiveness of procedures;
- preparation for external audits;
- identification of improvement areas;
- verification of corrective actions.

Internal auditors may understand the organisation deeply, but they must still maintain independence and avoid auditing their own work.

### Second-Party Audits

Second-party audits are performed by one organisation on another, usually by a customer auditing a supplier.

They are often used to verify whether a supplier meets contractual, security, quality or compliance requirements.

A second-party auditor may appear to have significant power because they represent the customer. For this reason, suggestions and findings must be handled carefully and ethically.

### Third-Party Audits

Third-party audits are performed by independent certification bodies.

They are typically more formal because their outcome may affect certification.

Third-party auditors must avoid consultancy and must not disclose knowledge obtained from other clients.

## 2. ISO 19011 Audit Process

ISO 19011 provides guidance for conducting audits. A typical audit process includes the following phases.

### Audit Initiation

Key activities include:

- assigning responsibility to the audit team leader;
- establishing contact with the auditee;
- confirming audit feasibility.

Feasibility depends on factors such as:

- availability of sufficient time and resources;
- availability of adequate information for planning;
- cooperation from the auditee;
- access to data and physical locations;
- ability to achieve the audit objectives.

### Preparation of Audit Activities

Preparation includes:

- reviewing documented information;
- planning the audit;
- applying a risk-based approach to planning;
- assigning work to audit team members;
- preparing working documents.

### Conducting Audit Activities

During the audit, the team:

- holds the opening meeting;
- confirms roles of guides and observers;
- communicates during the audit;
- collects and verifies information;
- reviews documented information;
- generates audit findings;
- determines audit conclusions;
- prepares the closing meeting.

### Reporting and Completion

The audit is completed by:

- preparing the audit report;
- distributing the report;
- formally closing the audit;
- performing follow-up activities where required.

## 3. Scope, Criteria and Objectives

### Audit Scope

The scope defines the boundaries of the audit.

It may be based on:

- geographic location;
- processes;
- departments;
- time period;
- organisational units;
- products or services.

### Audit Criteria

Audit criteria are the requirements against which evidence is evaluated.

They may include:

- ISO standards;
- legal, statutory or regulatory requirements;
- contractual requirements;
- industry codes of practice;
- internal policies and procedures;
- management system requirements;
- planned arrangements.

### Audit Objective

The objective defines why the audit is performed.

Possible objectives include:

- determining conformity with audit criteria;
- assessing whether processes meet management system requirements;
- evaluating the ability of the management system to meet legal and contractual obligations;
- assessing effectiveness in achieving objectives;
- identifying potential improvement areas.

## 4. Roles and Responsibilities

### Auditee Management

Auditee management should:

- provide adequate facilities;
- agree on the audit plan;
- ensure availability of auditees;
- resolve logistical issues;
- maintain responsibility for auditor safety;
- attend opening and closing meetings;
- act on audit report results.

### Auditees

Auditees should:

- cooperate with auditors;
- answer questions truthfully and respectfully;
- provide requested samples;
- avoid unnecessary distractions;
- support the achievement of audit objectives;
- help identify improvement areas.

### Audit Team Leader

The audit team leader is responsible for:

- planning the audit;
- using audit resources effectively;
- representing the audit team in communications;
- organising and directing the team;
- supporting auditors in training;
- leading the team to audit conclusions;
- preventing and resolving conflicts;
- preparing and completing the audit report;
- maintaining confidentiality.

### Auditors

Auditors should:

- support the team leader;
- prepare checklists or working documents;
- arrive on time;
- attend the opening meeting;
- perform assigned tasks;
- follow the audit schedule;
- document findings;
- keep the auditee informed;
- support report preparation;
- protect audit documents;
- maintain confidentiality.

### Guides and Observers

Guides and observers may:

- identify people to be interviewed;
- confirm schedules;
- organise access;
- ensure safety and security rules are known and followed;
- assist the audit on behalf of the auditee;
- clarify information or support evidence collection.

## 5. Audit Methods

Audit methods may vary depending on whether activities are on-site, remote, interactive or non-interactive.

### Human Interaction and On-Site Presence

Advantages:

- easier management of issues or questions;
- direct experience of real working conditions;
- stronger observation capability.

Disadvantages:

- little time to pause and reflect;
- possible distractions;
- risk of being drawn into areas outside the audit scope.

### Human Interaction and Remote Audit

Advantages:

- reduced travel time and logistics costs;
- possibility of more audits in a given period;
- less intrusive for the organisation.

Disadvantages:

- communication reliability issues;
- difficulty viewing documents clearly;
- possible delays or misunderstandings.

### No Human Interaction and On-Site Review

Advantages:

- auditors can review material without constant interruption;
- support is available if needed.

Disadvantages:

- risk of isolating the auditor;
- reduced understanding of how activities are actually performed;
- possible incorrect assumptions.

### No Human Interaction and Remote Review

Advantages:

- time for planning and reflection;
- reduced disruption to auditees;
- useful when combined with other audit methods.

Disadvantages:

- less effective when the auditor is unfamiliar with the organisation;
- obtaining clarifications may take longer.

## 6. Audit Planning Approaches

Different audit strategies may be used depending on the objective and context.

### Downstream or Top-Down Audit

This approach starts from a broad view of the organisation and follows processes downward.

It can be useful when comparing organisations or obtaining an overview of security controls.

Risk: the audit may become superficial if not supported by other techniques.

### Upstream Audit

This approach starts from an event, incident or issue and traces backwards through the processes and systems that enabled it.

It may provide detailed insight, but conclusions may be biased if the sample is too limited.

### Horizontal Audit

This approach reviews a specific control area across the organisation.

It may be useful in internal audits or when assessing all controls within a defined area.

### Combined Approach

A complete audit may combine multiple approaches depending on where the audit trail leads.

## 7. Opening Meeting

The opening meeting formally starts the audit and sets the tone for the audit relationship.

It should usually cover:

- introductions and attendance records;
- audit objective, scope and criteria;
- status of documented information;
- review of the audit plan;
- communication arrangements;
- availability of guides and observers;
- health, safety, security and emergency requirements;
- on-site activities that may affect the audit;
- logistics and resources;
- reporting methods and classification of findings;
- confidentiality;
- sampling approach;
- restrictions on physical or data access;
- closing meeting arrangements;
- complaints and appeals;
- final clarifications.

The opening meeting should be professional, clear and not unnecessarily long.

## 8. Objective Evidence

Objective evidence is information that supports audit findings.

It should be:

- factual;
- not influenced by emotion or bias;
- expressible;
- documentable;
- relevant to information security;
- auditable;
- based on documentation or observable phenomena.

### Evidence Triangle

Audit evidence usually comes from three sources:

1. interviews;
2. observations;
3. documented information.

Auditors should avoid relying only on verbal statements when documented or observable evidence is required.

Statements should also be admissible, meaning they should come from people who have responsibility and authority to provide them.

---

## 9. Effective Communication during Audits

Audit communication should support evidence collection and avoid creating unnecessary tension.

### Types of Questions

| Question Type | Purpose |
|---|---|
| Open | Encourages explanation and detail |
| Specific | Focuses on a particular responsibility or activity |
| Closed | Confirms a specific point |
| Hypothetical | Explores possible scenarios |
| Reflective | Confirms understanding |
| Probing | Investigates unclear or incomplete answers |
| Leading | Suggests an answer and should be used carefully |
| Rhetorical | Implies an answer and should generally be avoided |

A practical audit questioning flow can start with open questions and gradually move toward more specific, reflective and closed questions.

## 10. Audit Follow-Up

An audit report may identify the need for:

- corrections;
- corrective actions;
- preventive actions;
- improvement actions.

The auditee decides and implements actions within agreed timelines.

The auditor verifies whether actions are:

- timely;
- appropriate to the effects of the nonconformity;
- consistent with the organisation’s procedures;
- aligned with ISO 27001 requirements;
- effective in preventing recurrence.

### Follow-Up Process

A typical follow-up process may include:

1. identification of nonconformities;
2. summary report preparation;
3. corrective action request;
4. auditor review of the response;
5. completion of corrective actions by the auditee;
6. effectiveness review by the auditee;
7. auditor verification of completion;
8. escalation if necessary;
9. recording of each step.

## 11. Key Terminology

| Term | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Risk identification | Process of finding, recognising and describing risks |
| Continual improvement | Recurring activity to enhance performance |
| Information security | Preservation of confidentiality, integrity and availability |
| Process | Set of interrelated or interacting activities that transform inputs into outputs |
| Integrity | Property of accuracy and completeness |
| Nonconformity | Failure to satisfy a requirement |
| Risk analysis | Process to understand the nature of risk and determine its level |
| Corrective action | Action to eliminate the cause of a nonconformity and prevent recurrence |
| Correction | Action to eliminate a detected nonconformity |
| Availability | Property of being accessible and usable on demand by an authorised entity |
| Confidentiality | Property that information is not made available or disclosed to unauthorised entities |
| Risk evaluation | Comparison of risk analysis results with risk criteria |
| Risk treatment | Process to modify risk |
| Competence | Ability to apply knowledge and skills to achieve intended results |

## 12. ISMS Process Interactions

An ISMS should be understood as a set of interacting processes.

Key elements include:

- internal and external factors;
- interested parties;
- ISMS scope;
- leadership and management review;
- actions to address risks and opportunities;
- risk assessment;
- risk treatment;
- ISMS objectives;
- support processes;
- operations;
- monitoring, measurement, analysis and evaluation;
- performance evaluation;
- continual improvement.

## 13. Audit Initiation Questions

Before the audit, the auditor may need information from different parties.

### Audit Client

The audit client may clarify:

- audit objective;
- specific areas of focus;
- supplier selection concerns;
- previous incidents or events;
- communication issues;
- competence concerns;
- confidentiality, integrity or availability concerns.

### Audit Programme Manager

The programme manager may provide:

- audit team leader;
- team members and competences;
- audit objective;
- audit scope;
- audit criteria;
- audit methods;
- planned dates;
- audit duration;
- previous audit results.

### Audited Organisation Management

Audited organisation management should confirm:

- audit objective;
- audit scope;
- audit criteria;
- dates and duration;
- cooperation and access availability;
- relevant documentation;
- roles, responsibilities and authorities;
- number of employees and work shifts;
- whether a Stage 1 or familiarisation visit is needed.

---

## 14. Document Review

A document review checks whether required documented information exists and appears suitable for the audit scope.

Examples of ISO 27001 documentation or evidence include:

- ISMS scope;
- information security policy;
- information security risk assessment process;
- statement of applicability;
- risk treatment plan;
- risk treatment process;
- information security objectives;
- change planning;
- competence evidence;
- documented information required by the standard;
- externally originated documented information;
- evidence that processes were carried out as planned;
- results of risk assessments;
- results of risk treatment;
- monitoring and measurement evidence;
- audit programme and audit results;
- management review evidence;
- nonconformity and corrective action records.

The auditor should identify concerns to be followed up during interviews or later audit activities.

## 15. Working Document Examples

### Policy - Clause 5.2

Possible audit questions:

- How was the information security policy established?
- What evidence shows top management involvement?
- What is the organisation’s purpose and long-term strategy?
- Is the policy appropriate?
- Which requirements apply to the organisation and how does the policy address them?
- How is the policy reviewed?
- Does the policy include commitment to continual improvement?
- How is the policy communicated?

### Information Security Objectives - Clause 6.2

Possible audit questions:

- What is the process for setting and reviewing information security objectives?
- How is top management involved?
- What are the current objectives?
- Why were these objectives selected?
- How do objectives relate to policy, risk assessment and risk treatment?
- How are objectives measured?
- Have they been communicated?
- Is there evidence that objectives are evaluated or achieved?
- Who is responsible for actions related to objectives?

### Leadership and Commitment - Clause 5.1

Possible audit questions:

- Has top management demonstrated leadership and commitment?
- Are objectives and policy aligned with strategic direction?
- How are resources identified and authorised?
- What is top management’s daily involvement in the ISMS?
- What benefits have been obtained from the ISMS?
- How does management guide and support people involved in the ISMS?

## 16. Observational Audit Considerations

During a site visit, auditors may identify areas requiring further investigation.

Examples:

### Unattended Reception Area

Consider:

- visitor management;
- sign-in records;
- access to the rest of the building;
- supervision arrangements.

### Back Office Entrance

Consider:

- access control;
- emergency exit requirements;
- shared or exclusive parking access;
- physical security arrangements.

### Notice Board Data Charts

Consider:

- whether data is only monitoring/measurement;
- where analysis is performed;
- whether improvement opportunities are considered;
- whether actions have been defined.

### New Employees

Consider:

- ISMS policy awareness;
- role-specific competence;
- communication of roles, responsibilities and authorities;
- onboarding process.

### Confidential Waste Bins

Consider:

- whether bins are secure;
- evidence of proper use;
- emptying process;
- staff awareness.

### Open-Space Call Centre

Consider:

- overhearing of calls;
- clear desk and clear screen practices;
- user identification;
- call recording controls.

### Removable Media and Software

Consider:

- relevant policies;
- awareness and training;
- risk assessment;
- technical and organisational controls.

### Patch Panels

Consider:

- network diagrams;
- documentation;
- cabling security;
- access control.

## 17. Nonconformity Scenarios

Audit scenarios are used to apply ISO 27001 requirements to practical situations.

When analysing a scenario, consider:

1. What is the potential nonconformity?
2. What is the potential impact on the organisation?
3. Is there enough evidence to raise a nonconformity?
4. What additional evidence should be collected?

### Example Scenario Themes

- all selected controls come only from Annex A without considering other sources;
- inconsistent risk assessment results without organisational or risk appetite changes;
- information security objectives not aligned with top management’s strategic direction.

## 18. Closing Meeting

The closing meeting presents audit results and formally closes the audit.

The team leader should:

- restate the audit objective, scope and criteria;
- present the findings;
- explain nonconformities clearly;
- clarify timelines and next steps;
- avoid consultancy;
- avoid solving the issue for the auditee;
- ensure the organisation understands the results;
- close the meeting professionally.

### Behaviours to Avoid

Auditors should avoid:

- accepting gifts that may affect impartiality;
- allowing personal sympathy to influence findings;
- removing findings without objective justification;
- providing consultancy disguised as audit advice;
- recommending specific commercial solutions;
- disclosing information about other clients;
- making certification decisions based on superficial impressions.

---

## 19. Audit Report

The audit report should record the official outcome of the audit.

It should include:

- audit objectives;
- scope;
- criteria;
- client identification;
- audit team;
- participants;
- audit dates;
- findings;
- conclusions;
- conformity statements;
- nonconformities, where applicable.

A separate nonconformity report may be used to support corrective action management.

---

## 20. Example Nonconformity Themes

### Segregation of Duties

Potential issue:

- one role is responsible for evaluation, supplier negotiation, business case approval and budget approval without restrictions.

Relevant concept:

- conflicting duties and responsibilities should be segregated to reduce opportunities for unauthorised or unintended misuse of organisational assets.

### Review of Information Security Policies

Potential issue:

- policies have not been reviewed within defined intervals or after significant organisational change.

Relevant concept:

- information security policies should be reviewed at planned intervals or when significant changes occur.

### Disciplinary Procedure

Potential issue:

- disciplinary procedures exist but are not communicated or accessible to personnel.

Relevant concept:

- there should be a formal and communicated disciplinary process for information security violations.

### Return of Assets

Potential issue:

- assets assigned to former employees cannot be traced and return evidence is missing.

Relevant concept:

- employees and external users should return organisational assets at the end of employment, contract or agreement.

### Clear Desk and Clear Screen

Potential issue:

- confidential documents are left visible on unattended desks despite the policy being communicated.

Relevant concept:

- clear desk and clear screen practices should be adopted and followed.
