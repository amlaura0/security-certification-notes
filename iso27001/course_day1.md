## Audit Programme and Audit Plan

One of the first fundamental concepts covered in the course concerns the distinction between an “audit programme” and an “audit plan”. In everyday language these terms are often used as synonyms, but in ISO 19011 they have completely different meanings.

The audit programme represents the general planning of audits over time. In practice, the audit programme can be compared to a strategic calendar of the auditor’s or certification body’s activities. For example, establishing that a company will be audited in July 2026 falls within the audit programme. At this stage, the operational details of the audit are not yet defined; only the period and the general planning of the activities are established.

The audit plan, on the other hand, concerns the operational detail of the audit days. Once the dates have been established within the programme, the plan precisely defines which activities will be carried out, at what times, by which auditors, and with which company representatives involved. For example, the plan may include an opening meeting from 8:30 to 9:00, an interview with top management from 9:00 to 11:00, and the review of the sales department in the following hours. The audit plan is therefore an operational tool that makes it possible to organise fieldwork in practical terms and to ensure that all planned areas are properly assessed.

## Types of Audit

### First-Party Audits

There are different types of audits under ISO standards: first-party, second-party, and third-party audits. First-party audits are internal audits, meaning the checks that an organisation carries out on itself to verify the conformity of its management system with regulatory requirements. In the context of ISO 27001, this means verifying that processes, procedures, controls, and activities are consistent with the requirements of the information security standard.

A fundamental principle of internal audits is the independence of the auditor. A person cannot audit their own department or their own activities, because this would compromise the impartiality of the judgement.

In smaller organisations this can be difficult, especially when there is a limited number of qualified employees. Once the qualification has been obtained, it is possible to train other internal auditors within the company, thereby creating an independent verification system even in smaller business contexts.

### Second-Party Audits

Second-party audits are audits carried out on suppliers. In this case, an organisation checks external parties from which it receives products or services in order to verify that they meet certain security, quality, or regulatory compliance requirements.

The objective of a second-party audit is to ensure that the supplier operates securely and in line with the customer’s expectations.

### Third-Party Audits

Third-party audits are audits carried out by independent certification bodies. Their objective is to issue ISO 27001 certification and officially attest that the organisation’s management system complies with the requirements of the standard. However, attending a Lead Auditor course does not mean immediately becoming a third-party auditor. To obtain this qualification, additional requirements must be met:

- at least three years of experience are required in the sector one wishes to audit;
- practical experience must be gained by participating in real audits as observers, that is, as auditors working alongside qualified auditors.

Normally, a person must participate in numerous field audit days before obtaining full qualification. Only after accumulating sufficient practical experience can a certification body officially authorise a person to operate as a third-party auditor.

## The Resources Needed for an Audit

### The Role of the Auditor and the Technical Expert

An auditor may find themselves auditing organisations that belong to sectors completely different from their own professional background. For example, an auditor who has always worked in the banking or IT sector may be assigned to carry out an audit at a steel company. In situations of this kind there are two options: decline the assignment or accept it with the support of a technical expert.

The technical expert does not replace the auditor and does not directly conduct the audit, but provides specialist support during the verification activities. Their task is to help the audit team understand particularly complex technical aspects of the sector being audited. The technical expert may intervene, for example, when the audited organisation uses highly specialised industrial processes or when there are specific regulatory requirements that require specialised expertise. In this way, the audit maintains its effectiveness even in very different contexts.

## The Composition of the Audit Team

### Team Leader and Co-Teamers

An audit may be carried out by a single auditor or by an entire work group. The main person responsible is the team leader, that is, the auditor who coordinates all activities. The team leader is responsible for planning, communication with the company, management of the audit plan, conducting the opening and closing meetings, and drafting the final report.

Alongside the team leader, other auditors may operate; these are called co-teamers. The need to use more than one auditor mainly depends on the size of the audited organisation. ISO 17021 in fact provides precise criteria for calculating the number of audit days required on the basis of the number of employees, work shifts, and organisational complexity. If the required number of days exceeds the standard working week, the only solution is to distribute the work among several auditors operating in parallel.

In large industrial organisations, the audit team can become very large, even reaching ten auditors at the same time. In these cases, the coordination work of the team leader becomes essential, because they must ensure that all verification activities are consistent and that the information collected is correctly integrated into the final report.

### Observers

Within the audit group there may also be other figures in addition to the auditors themselves. One of these is the observer, meaning a person in training who attends the audit without actively participating. The observer does not ask questions and does not intervene directly, but follows the team’s work in order to learn how a real audit is carried out. This shadowing phase is essential for gaining practical experience and becoming a qualified auditor.

### Witness Auditor

Another important figure is the witness auditor. This is an auditor sent by Accredia or by the accreditation body to check the behaviour of the audit team. The witness auditor does not audit the company being audited, but observes the way in which the auditors conduct the audit. The purpose is not punitive, but aimed at continual improvement. Through the witness audit, it is verified that auditors comply with the rules set out in ISO 19011 and maintain correct, impartial, and professional behaviour.

## Documented Information

Documented information represents the core of audit activity and makes the difference between a competent auditor and an unprepared one.

Documented information consists of all objective evidence used to demonstrate conformity of the management system. It may include:

- procedures;
- policies;
- records;
- reports;
- logs;
- configurations;
- technical documents;
- operational evidence.

The auditor uses this information to verify that what the organisation declares is actually applied in practice. Without documented evidence, it is not possible to demonstrate conformity with the requirements of the standard.

## Combined Audits and Joint Audits

### Combined Audits

A combined audit consists of the simultaneous assessment of several standards within the same organisation. For example, it is possible to carry out an ISO 27001 audit and an ISO 45001 audit during the same period.

This approach makes it possible to optimise time and costs. Instead of carrying out two completely separate audits, the organisation concentrates the activities in a single period, reducing the operational impact and overall costs. This solution can also be advantageous for certification bodies, because it allows them to build customer loyalty by offering multiple certifications at the same time.

### Joint Audits

A joint audit, on the other hand, is a different situation. In this case, several organisations participate together in the audit of the same company. This occurs especially in second-party audits, when a problem involves not only the direct supplier but also other parties in the supply chain. Two companies may therefore present themselves together to audit a common supplier. A witness audit must not be confused with a joint audit, because the witness auditor does not audit the company, but the behaviour of the auditors themselves.

## Compliance and Conformity

Conformity represents a voluntary choice. An organisation independently decides to adopt a standard, such as ISO 27001, for strategic, commercial, or organisational reasons. For example, a company may choose to become certified in order to participate in public tenders, improve its reputation, or increase customer trust.

Compliance, instead, represents a legal obligation. In the case of the NIS2 Directive, some organisations are legally required to comply with certain cybersecurity requirements. It is therefore not a voluntary choice, but a regulatory obligation whose non-compliance may result in very heavy penalties.

ISO 27001 may represent a form of preventive protection with respect to regulatory obligations. Since the ISO standard often requires stricter controls than those provided for by law, an ISO 27001-certified organisation is generally in a much safer position also with respect to the requirements of NIS2.

## The Role of the Guide during the Audit

The guide is a person belonging to the audited organisation who accompanies the audit team during all activities. Their main task is to assist the auditors in moving around the company, facilitate access to areas, and coordinate the people involved in the interviews.

In large organisations, the guide plays a fundamental role because they allow auditors to orient themselves among offices, departments, and complex structures. However, their task is not limited to logistics. The guide also intervenes to manage any tense situations and maintain a collaborative atmosphere between the auditors and company personnel.

## Initial Certification and the Two Audit Stages

### Stage 1: Document Review Audit

Stage 1 is a primarily documentary audit and its purpose is to verify the maturity of the management system before the actual audit. The auditor physically visits the company, becomes familiar with the environment, learns about the production processes, and collects preliminary information on how the organisation works.

The main objective of Stage 1 is to establish whether the management system is mature enough to face Stage 2. During this stage, fundamental aspects are checked, such as:

- the scope of the system;
- the purpose of the certification;
- mandatory documentation;
- risk analysis;
- the statement of applicability;
- system policies;
- management review.

During Stage 1, no formal nonconformities are issued. The auditor may instead issue observations, that is, reports highlighting gaps or weaknesses in the management system.

These observations have a preventive function. They serve to warn the organisation that certain elements must be corrected before Stage 2. For example, if during the review the statement of applicability has not been completed, the auditor does not immediately issue a nonconformity, but reports the problem as an observation. These observations represent a kind of “advance warning”. If the same problems are still present during Stage 2, they will then be transformed into actual nonconformities. In other words, Stage 1 gives the organisation an opportunity to correct gaps before the official certification audit.

### Stage 2: The Certification Audit

Stage 2 represents the actual certification audit. If Stage 1 has had a positive outcome, the organisation is considered ready to face the complete assessment of the management system.

The purpose of Stage 2 is to assess:

- the implementation of the system;
- the effectiveness of the system.

The difference between these two concepts is very important. An “implemented” system is a system that truly exists and is applied in daily practice. An “effective” system, on the other hand, is a system that actually achieves the expected results.

This means that having well-written documents is not enough: the organisation must demonstrate that the processes really work and that the security controls produce concrete results.

### The Three-Year Certification Cycle

Once initial certification has been obtained, the organisation enters a continuous audit cycle lasting three years.

The typical cycle consists of:

- initial audit (Stage 1 + Stage 2);
- first annual surveillance audit;
- second annual surveillance audit;
- recertification audit.

After recertification, the cycle starts again.

Stage 1 is carried out only during the first initial certification and is no longer repeated in subsequent years. Surveillance audits, on the other hand, are intended to verify that the system continues to be properly maintained over time. During recertification, instead, the auditor once again reviews all processes of the management system.

## The Opening Meeting

Stage 2 formally begins with the opening meeting. This activity is described by ISO 19011 and represents a fundamental moment of the audit.

During the opening meeting:

- the auditors are introduced;
- the audit plan is confirmed;
- any organisational changes are checked;
- the audit objectives are clarified;
- operational and logistical arrangements are defined.

The opening meeting also serves to create a collaborative atmosphere between the auditor and the audited organisation. The course strongly emphasises the importance of the relational approach: the auditor must not adopt an aggressive or inquisitorial attitude, but must establish a professional and respectful relationship with company personnel.

## The Final Report and Nonconformities

At the end of the audit, a final report is drafted summarising the activities carried out and the results that emerged. The report may include:

- conformities;
- observations;
- nonconformities.

Nonconformities are normally managed through specific separate reports, in which the organisation must describe:

- the causes of the problem;
- corrective actions;
- resolution timelines.

The objective is not to punish the company, but to ensure the continual improvement of the management system.

## Checklists and the Process Approach

### Moving Beyond Traditional Checklists

In the past, audits were mainly conducted using very rigid checklists: long lists of questions to which the auditor asked for specific answers.

However, this approach had several limitations:

- excessively long audits;
- excessive rigidity;
- risk of overlooking aspects not included in the checklist;
- an approach perceived as an interrogation.

Today this approach is considered outdated.

### The Process Approach

The modern approach instead consists of following business processes and asking open-ended questions. The auditor no longer uses a rigid sequence of questions, but invites people to freely explain their work.

Typical questions may include:

- “Can you explain how your process works?”
- “What happens when this event occurs?”
- “How do you manage this activity in daily practice?”

This method produces many advantages:

- it makes interviewees feel at ease;
- it makes the audit more natural;
- it makes it possible to collect much more information;
- it facilitates the spontaneous identification of nonconformities.

Often, nonconformities emerge directly from the accounts of the people interviewed, without the need for rigid interrogations or forced questions.

### Sampling during the Audit

The auditor cannot analyse 100% of the company’s documentation. For this reason, sampling is used during audits. Sampling consists of selecting a number of representative examples to be checked in detail. A sample of three or five examples is generally considered sufficient to obtain reliable evidence.

Through sampling, the auditor can assess:

- the correct application of procedures;
- the consistency of records;
- compliance with the controls provided for by the management system.

This approach makes it possible to keep the audit sustainable for both the auditor and the audited organisation.

### The Audit Opening Meeting

The first activity carried out during an audit is the opening meeting. This meeting represents the official start of the on-site audit and has the purpose of confirming all operational arrangements under which the verification activities will be carried out. The auditor normally sends the audit plan to the organisation about two weeks before the start of the activities, but the opening meeting is used to verify that what has been planned is still actually applicable.

During this stage, organisational contingencies may arise. For example, a person who was supposed to be interviewed may be absent. In such cases, the audit plan is not considered immutable, but may be changed through shared agreements between the auditor and the audited organisation. Interviews may be swapped, postponed, or assigned to substitute figures, provided that the coverage of the planned processes is still guaranteed.

The opening meeting also includes the introduction of all participants. The team leader introduces the members of the audit team, any observers, technical experts, or witness auditors, while the organisation introduces the managers of the business processes that will be involved in the verification activities.

## The Table Metaphor

### The Process Approach: From the Pyramid Structure to the Horizontal Vision

One of the most important concepts of the entire course is the process approach, considered one of the fundamental pillars of all modern ISO standards. Traditionally, companies are represented through a pyramidal organisation chart: general management is at the top, below it are managers, and subsequently employees and operators. This representation reflects a classic hierarchical structure, in which decision-making power descends from the top down.

ISO, however, introduces a completely different perspective. While it does not eliminate the company hierarchy, the process approach transforms the organisation into a network of activities connected horizontally. The company is no longer seen primarily as a command pyramid, but as a set of processes that collaborate to produce value for the customer.

The table metaphor is used to explain this concept. The old pyramid is “transformed” into a table made up of a horizontal surface and supporting legs. The surface represents customer-oriented processes, while the legs represent the support processes needed to keep the organisation standing.

### Customer-Oriented Processes: The Customer as the Centre of the System

According to the ISO philosophy, the true centre of the organisation is the customer. The entire business system is designed with the objective of providing value to the end customer. This logic derives directly from ISO 9001 and has also been inherited by ISO 27001.

Customer-oriented processes are therefore all those processes that directly contribute to the creation of the product or service requested by the customer. In the table metaphor, they constitute the upper surface.

### 1st PROCESS: The Sales Process

The first customer-oriented process is sales. Before producing anything, a company must in fact find customers. Without customers, there is no economic reason for the organisation to exist.

Sales is responsible for:

- identifying the market;
- contacting customers;
- collecting requests;
- understanding the customer’s needs.

From a logical point of view, this is the first process needed to start business activity. Even in consulting companies or service organisations, the sales process represents the starting point of the entire organisational chain.

### 2nd PROCESS: Industrialisation or Feasibility Analysis

Once the customer’s request has been received, the second process comes into play: industrialisation, also called the technical office or feasibility analysis.

This process must answer two fundamental questions:

1. Is the company technically able to meet the request?
2. Is it economically worthwhile to do so?

Industrialisation therefore assesses:

- technical feasibility;
- costs;
- necessary resources;
- timeframes;
- economic sustainability.

Only after this analysis can sales formulate a final offer to the customer.

### 3rd PROCESS: Inbound Logistics

After approval of the project, it becomes necessary to obtain everything needed to produce the requested service or product. This task belongs to inbound logistics.

Inbound logistics manages:

- arrival of raw materials;
- availability of resources;
- supplies;
- licences;
- infrastructures;
- tools needed for production.

In the case of a manufacturing company, this concerns the arrival of physical materials; in the case of a service, it may involve software, licences, connections, or technological tools.

### 4th PROCESS: The Production Process

The fourth process is production itself. In this phase, the organisation concretely creates the product or provides the service requested by the customer.

Production transforms the inputs received from the previous processes into a final output. For example, a consulting company provides a service or a training organisation delivers a course.

Production therefore represents the operational core of the company.

### 5th PROCESS: Outbound Logistics

The last customer-oriented process is outbound logistics. Once the good or service has been produced, it must be delivered to the final customer.

Outbound logistics manages:

- shipments;
- distribution;
- delivery of services;
- transmission of results to the customer.

In this way the cycle closes by returning once again to the customer, who represents both the starting point and the end point of the entire business system.

### Support Processes: The Role of the “Table Legs”

If customer-oriented processes represent the surface of the table, support processes instead represent the legs that keep it standing. These processes do not directly generate economic value, but they are essential for the company to function.

### 1st leg: Procurement

The first support process is procurement. This process deals with obtaining all the resources needed by the company. The process consists of:

- selecting suppliers;
- purchasing materials;
- managing commercial relationships;
- enabling the arrival of the necessary resources.

Although these activities do not directly produce value, they represent an essential function for the organisation’s existence.

### 2nd leg: Management

The second leg of the table is management. Management represents the strategic component of the organisation and defines:

- objectives;
- strategies;
- guidelines;
- business decisions.

In the ISO approach, management is no longer seen as the “absolute top” of the pyramid, but as a support function that sustains the entire organisation. Its task is to guide, orient, and provide commitment to the management system.

### 3rd leg: Human Resources

The third leg of the table is represented by human resources. This process manages:

- personnel selection;
- training;
- competencies;
- organisation of roles;
- management of workers.

The course emphasises that human resources represent one of the most complex and costly aspects of business management, but at the same time they are absolutely indispensable for the organisation’s functioning.

### 4th leg: The Management System

The fourth leg of the table is the management system itself. This function defines:

- procedures;
- operating instructions;
- methodologies;
- controls;
- organisational rules.

The management system manager translates the strategic objectives of management into concrete and controlled processes. This is the person who establishes “how” the organisation must operate in order to achieve the objectives defined by management.

In smaller companies, this function may coincide directly with the owner or general manager, while in larger organisations it is entrusted to dedicated figures such as the quality manager or the management system manager.

### Bidirectional Relationships in Support Processes

Unlike customer-oriented processes, which mainly follow a logical linear sequence, support processes operate through bidirectional relationships. This means that a process may be both a customer and a supplier of other business processes at the same time. For example:

- management provides strategic guidelines to the other processes;
- but at the same time it receives information and support from process owners;
- the management system defines procedures;
- but it depends on human resources and operational activities to apply them concretely.

This network of interactions is directly referenced in Chapters 4 and 5 of ISO standards, where organisational context, leadership, and interactions between processes are discussed. The business system is therefore interpreted as a dynamic set of collaborative relationships rather than as a simple vertical hierarchy.

## Mapping Business Processes

There is no fixed number of business processes. The nine processes described through the table metaphor represent only a minimum reference structure:

- five customer-oriented processes;
- four support processes.

In reality, each organisation may introduce additional processes based on its complexity and operational needs. Some examples may include:

- IT;
- administration;
- legal office;
- research and development;
- compliance;
- marketing;
- document management.

What matters is not the absolute number of processes, but the fact that each process has:

- defined objectives;
- clear responsibilities;
- identified interactions;
- a process owner responsible for its functioning.
### The Concept of Process Owner

Every business process must have a person responsible for it, called the process owner. The process owner manages their process as if it were a small autonomous company.

Their responsibilities include:

- supervising activities;
- controlling outputs;
- managing interactions with other processes;
- continual improvement;
- achievement of objectives.

In larger companies, each process normally has a dedicated manager. In smaller organisations, however, a single person may hold several roles at the same time.

### The Strategic Role of Management

In the traditional pyramidal vision, management is at the absolute top of the organisation. In the process approach, however, management becomes a strategic support function that sustains and coordinates the business system.

Its main task consists of:

- defining objectives;
- understanding the context;
- guiding change;
- ensuring resources;
- promoting continual improvement.

According to the instructor, many companies fail not because of sudden crises, but because management is unable to understand the context in which it operates and does not adapt to external changes. The ability to adapt is therefore central. An example is the Nokia case and the physical keyboard: the inability to adapt quickly to technological evolution progressively led the company to lose its market leadership.

## Risk-Based Thinking

### The Second Pillar of ISO Standards

According to this philosophy, an organisation must not simply carry out operational activities, but must continuously:

- identify risks;
- assess threats;
- seize opportunities;
- anticipate future problems.

The company therefore cannot merely execute operational procedures mechanically. Instead, it must develop a continuous capacity for analysis and forecasting.

Risk-based thinking as preventive analysis: identifying a potential risk and adopting preventive measures before the problem manifests itself irreversibly.

### The Relationship between Objectives and Risk Analysis

An organisation must first clearly define its objectives and then identify all factors that could influence their achievement. Risk analysis therefore serves to:

- protect business objectives;
- prevent negative events;
- identify opportunities;
- support strategic decisions.

This principle is present in all the main modern ISO standards.

### Root Cause and the Cause-Effect Principle

When any of the following occurs:

- a nonconformity;
- a security incident;
- a customer complaint;
- an operational error;

the organisation must seek the root cause of the problem and not limit itself to correcting only the superficial effects. This principle represents one of the foundations of continual improvement required by ISO management systems.

### Context Analysis and Risk Treatment

One of the fundamental aspects introduced by risk-based thinking is the need to analyse the context in which the organisation operates. The company cannot limit itself to looking at its internal processes, but must also understand all external factors that could influence the achievement of the management system objectives.

Example: an organisation located in a seismic area. An earthquake cannot be predicted with certainty, but this does not mean that the risk should be ignored. The organisation may in fact adopt several preventive strategies:

- constructing earthquake-resistant buildings;
- preparing evacuation plans;
- protecting information through geographic backups;
- creating alternative sites in areas less exposed to the risk.

These activities represent concrete examples of risk treatment. In some cases the risk is mitigated, meaning reduced through controls and preventive measures. In other cases the risk is avoided completely, for example by transferring infrastructures or activities to safer locations.

### Chapter 4 and Chapter 6 of ISO 27001

The course highlights that the core of ISO 27001 is represented above all by:

- Chapter 4 → context analysis;
- Chapter 6 → risk management and planning.

These chapters allow the organisation to understand:

- which threats exist;
- which opportunities are present;
- which controls must be implemented;
- how to protect the management system over time.

The entire logic of ISO 27001 revolves around risk assessment and the organisation’s ability to continuously adapt to changes in the context.

## Risks in the Opening Meeting

The opening meeting is also reinterpreted through the logic of risk-based thinking. The auditor must be able to identify possible critical issues from the very first moments of the audit.

Examples of risks mentioned in the course:

- company videos that are too long and consume the available time;
- excessively long lunches;
- artificially prepared presentations;
- evidence selected specifically to “appear perfect”;
- absence of process owners;
- missing documentation;
- lack of guides for the auditors.

The auditor must therefore always maintain a critical and professional attitude, avoiding being influenced by overly constructed representations of the company’s reality.

## The Triangle of Objective Evidence

One of the most important concepts introduced in the course is the triangle of objective evidence. According to this logic, the auditor collects evidence through three main sources.

### 1. What the Auditor Hears

The first source of evidence is represented by the statements of the people interviewed. During interviews, the auditor collects information through:

- explanations;
- accounts;
- operational descriptions;
- answers to questions.

This information constitutes an important part of the audit evidence.

### 2. What the Auditor Observes

The second source is direct observation. The auditor checks with their own eyes:

- environments;
- behaviours;
- configurations;
- operational activities;
- physical elements.

For example, if a company states that it has a policy posted on the notice board but the auditor does not actually find it displayed, this becomes objective evidence of nonconformity.

### 3. Documented Information

The third source of evidence consists of documented information:

- procedures;
- records;
- policies;
- reports;
- configurations;
- logs;
- official documents.

For some requirements of the standard, the presence of documented information is mandatory. In these cases, if the document is missing, the nonconformity is automatic and cannot be compensated for by simple verbal statements.
